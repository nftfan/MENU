Why Donald Trump Wants Greenland

Strategic, Economic & Geopolitical Reasons Explained

Key Takeaways

  • Strategic military positioning in the Arctic
  • Competing with Russia and China
  • Securing access to valuable mineral resources
  • Leveraging new Arctic shipping lanes due to climate change
  • Conflicts with Greenlandic sovereignty and Danish authority

Former U.S. President Donald Trump and members of his administration have repeatedly discussed Greenland, sparking headlines about a potential purchase. While the idea made waves in 2019, the reasoning involves strategic, economic, and geopolitical factors that have long shaped U.S. Arctic policy.

1. Strategic Military Importance

Greenland sits in a highly strategic Arctic location between North America, Europe, and Russia. This position is crucial for monitoring airspace, naval routes, and missile defense systems. The U.S. already maintains a major military presence at Pituffik Space Base (formerly Thule Air Base), and controlling Greenland could extend American reach deeper into the Arctic.

2. National Security & Great Power Competition

Trump and his allies view Greenland as vital to deterring rival powers, particularly Russia and China, who are increasing Arctic activities. Controlling the island would prevent rivals from establishing bases, infrastructure, or influence near the U.S., enhancing American strategic positioning.

3. Natural Resources & Economic Potential

Greenland is rich in minerals, including rare earth elements, uranium, copper, zinc, and possibly oil and gas. Rare earth minerals are especially significant because China dominates global production, and securing alternative supplies would benefit U.S. defense, technology, and renewable energy industries.

4. Climate Change Opens New Opportunities

Melting Arctic ice is opening new shipping lanes and access to previously unreachable resources. Greenland’s location could position the U.S. to benefit from emerging Arctic commerce and resource exploitation.

5. Historical Context

The U.S. has long eyed Greenland. In 1946, President Harry Truman offered $100 million in gold to purchase it, but Denmark refused. Trump revived this idea publicly in 2019, sometimes framing it as a “national security necessity.” However, Greenland remains an autonomous part of the Kingdom of Denmark.

Greenland is not for sale. Any sovereignty change would require consent from Greenland’s people and constitutional changes in Denmark, making a purchase highly unlikely under international law.
